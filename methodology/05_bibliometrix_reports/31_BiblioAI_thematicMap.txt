### Understanding the Strategic Map Structure

The strategic map plots thematic clusters based on two key dimensions:

*   **Relevance Degree (Centrality) - X-axis:** This measures how strongly a cluster is connected to other clusters in the network. Clusters with high centrality are crucial for the overall structure of the field, acting as central hubs of research.
*   **Development Degree (Density) - Y-axis:** This measures the internal cohesion and development of a cluster. Clusters with high density are well-developed, with strong links between their constituent keywords, indicating a focused and mature research area.

These two dimensions define four quadrants, each representing a different type of thematic development:

1.  **Motor Themes (Top-Right):** High Centrality, High Density. These are well-developed and centrally important themes that drive the field's progress. They are both cohesive internally and strongly connected to other research areas.
2.  **Niche Themes (Top-Left):** Low Centrality, High Density. These themes are internally well-developed and focused but have fewer connections to the broader field. They represent specialized or emerging areas that are gaining internal coherence but haven't yet significantly influenced the larger discourse.
3.  **Basic Themes (Bottom-Right):** High Centrality, Low Density. These are foundational themes that are highly connected to many other areas (central) but lack strong internal cohesion (low density). They might represent broad, long-standing concepts or methods that underpin the field but are not themselves areas of intense, focused development.
4.  **Emerging or Declining Themes (Bottom-Left):** Low Centrality, Low Density. These themes are neither well-developed internally nor strongly connected externally. They could be new, nascent areas (emerging) or older topics that are losing research interest (declining).
